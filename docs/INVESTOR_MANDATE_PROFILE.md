# Investor Mandate And Profile

## Scope Of This File

This file defines how EstateLab profiles a user before applying the property-selection framework.

It should not copy the founder's personal financial profile into every user. Instead, it should use founder insight to interpret user behavior, cash reserve, risk tolerance, strategy fit, and danger signs.

Use `docs/MY_INVESTMENT_FRAMEWORK.md` to judge a property. Use this file to judge whether the user is suitable for the strategy being considered.

## Core Thesis

The same property can be suitable for one investor and unsuitable for another.

EstateLab should first identify the investor's mandate, financial resilience, knowledge level, and likely mistake pattern. Then it should decide which strategies are allowed, restricted, or blocked.

The central question is:

> Can this investor survive the strategy long enough for the thesis to work?

## Founder Market Observation

Most Malaysian property investors encountered by the founder are retail investors.

Retail investors often lack professional due diligence process, rely heavily on agent narratives, and may judge affordability based on loan approval rather than actual holding power.

EstateLab should therefore protect retail investors from:

- Buying only because the bank allows it.
- Assuming high income equals investment competence.
- Ignoring cash reserve.
- Being influenced by stronger personalities, agents, or group narratives.
- Buying before comparing substitutes.
- Overestimating the price they can safely afford.
- Treating property as safe without testing rent, resale, title, and cash-flow stress.

## Most Dangerous Profile

The most dangerous investor profile is not necessarily low income. The founder views the most dangerous profile as:

> High income but does not understand what they are buying, and proceeds mainly because they can afford it.

This investor may pass financing checks while still making a poor investment decision.

EstateLab should separate:

- **Borrowing capacity**: what the bank may lend.
- **Affordability**: what the user can pay.
- **Holding power**: what the user can survive during vacancy, repairs, rate changes, or delayed exit.
- **Investment competence**: whether the user understands the asset, risk, and exit.

High income reduces cash-flow pressure, but it does not fix weak judgment.

## Buying Power Discipline

A user is overestimating buying power when they take a loan above what they can comfortably pay.

EstateLab should not trust the user's stated budget at face value. Founder default:

> Assume the user's practical budget is at least 10% lower than the stated amount until verified.

EstateLab should verify:

- Income stability.
- Existing commitments.
- Emergency fund.
- Cash after down payment and transaction costs.
- Renovation and furnishing budget.
- Vacancy and repair buffer.
- Interest-rate sensitivity.
- Whether the user can still survive if rent is delayed or lower than expected.

Loan approval is not proof of investment readiness.

## Cash Reserve Gate

EstateLab should require a minimum emergency fund before encouraging a user to proceed.

Founder default:

- Minimum cash buffer: 6 months of salary.

This buffer should be separate from purchase cash outlay, renovation, furnishing, legal fees, stamp duty, maintenance arrears, and other transaction costs.

Users with low income and no cash reserve should avoid property investment for now.

## Strategy Fit By Investor Profile

### High-Rise Rental Property

High-rise rental property is more suitable for investors with lower cash reserve than landed investors, provided the unit can support rental holding power.

EstateLab should still require:

- Rent that can cover installment under the user mandate.
- Vacancy buffer.
- Maintenance and sinking fund allowance.
- Tenant segment fit.
- Manageable repair and furnishing cost.
- Exit buyer bankability.

### Landed Appreciation Property

Landed appreciation property is more suitable for investors with high cash reserve.

This is because landed property may produce weaker rental yield, require stronger holding power, and depend more on long-term capital appreciation.

EstateLab should allow negative cash flow only when:

- The user is cash-rich.
- The landed appreciation thesis is credible.
- The shortfall is within tolerance.
- The user can survive an extended holding period.
- The downside case does not force a distressed sale.

### Installment-Covering Strategy

Normal retail investors should generally buy only properties where rent can cover installment.

This rule protects users who do not have deep cash reserve, professional asset-management capability, or the ability to absorb long negative-cash-flow periods.

## Investor Profile Classification

### Conservative

A conservative investor has limited risk tolerance and should prioritize survival, liquidity, and low mistake cost.

Typical signs:

- Limited cash reserve beyond emergency fund.
- Fear of vacancy or bad tenant.
- Needs rent to cover installment.
- Lower tolerance for renovation, legal complication, or illiquidity.
- Prefers proven areas and straightforward title.

Allowed strategy:

- High-rise rental in mature demand areas.
- Strong rent coverage.
- Clean title and strong bankability.
- Avoid speculative growth corridors and negative cash flow.

### Balanced

A balanced investor has enough reserve to take measured risk but still needs evidence discipline.

Typical signs:

- Has emergency fund plus acquisition buffer.
- Can tolerate small temporary cash-flow friction.
- Wants both rent support and resale upside.
- Can compare alternatives rationally.
- Does not need the fastest exit but still cares about liquidity.

Allowed strategy:

- Quality high-rise with rent support and resale depth.
- Selective growth areas with clear catalyst.
- Mild renovation or furnishing value-add.
- Avoid unresolved hard stops.

### Growth-Focused

A growth-focused investor can accept lower current yield for stronger future appreciation, but only with adequate holding power.

Typical signs:

- Higher cash reserve.
- Stable income.
- Longer holding period.
- Able to tolerate controlled negative cash flow.
- Understands catalyst, supply, and exit timing.

Allowed strategy:

- Landed appreciation property.
- Growth corridors with credible master developer.
- Prime scarcity plays.
- Lower yield only when the appreciation thesis is evidence-backed.

### Cash-Flow-Focused

A cash-flow-focused investor prioritizes rent, stability, and monthly holding power.

Typical signs:

- Wants rent to cover installment and recurring costs.
- Values tenant stability.
- Less dependent on capital appreciation.
- Will accept slower resale if rent is strong.

Allowed strategy:

- Rental-led high-rise.
- Mature demand locations.
- Fully furnished units with tenant urgency.
- Possible illiquidity only when cash flow strongly compensates.

### Opportunistic

An opportunistic investor can act on special situations but must have skill and reserve.

Typical signs:

- Strong cash reserve.
- Able to analyze discounts and defects.
- Understands legal, title, auction, renovation, or distressed-sale process.
- Can walk away when the discount is not real.

Allowed strategy:

- Discounted units.
- Remaining developer stock.
- Auction opportunities.
- Value-add renovation.
- Only when evidence proves the asset is undervalued, not cheap for a reason.

### Speculative Or Unsafe

An unsafe investor should be blocked or strongly discouraged.

Typical signs:

- Low income and no cash reserve.
- Wants to buy mainly because others are buying.
- Takes loan beyond comfortable payment ability.
- Does not understand the property.
- Is ambitious but lacks knowledge.
- Is easily manipulated by opinions.
- Relies on optimistic rent, future appreciation, or financing tricks.
- Wants to proceed without comparison.
- Ignores hard stops.

EstateLab should not encourage this user to buy. The correct recommendation is to build cash reserve, knowledge, and decision discipline first.

## Marked-Up Or Cash-Out Vulnerability

The founder observes that poorer or cash-stressed investors are more likely to be trapped by marked-up or cash-out narratives.

EstateLab should refine this carefully:

The vulnerable profile is not simply "poor people"; it is a user with low cash reserve, urgent liquidity need, weak financial literacy, and strong desire to start without enough capital.

EstateLab should be extra cautious when a user:

- Needs cash out more than they need the property.
- Cannot fund transaction costs normally.
- Does not understand the real debt burden.
- Focuses on immediate cash instead of property quality.
- Relies on other people's reassurance.
- Treats financing as the investment thesis.

## Emotional-Chasing Warning

A user may be emotionally chasing a deal when they want to purchase without doing comparison.

EstateLab should flag:

- No substitute comparison.
- Refusal to check transaction evidence.
- Ignoring cheaper or better alternatives.
- Repeating agent claims without evidence.
- Treating urgency as proof of opportunity.
- Moving from excitement to commitment before due diligence.

## Signs Of Real Investor Edge

EstateLab should not assume every user has an edge.

Potential real edges include:

- Deep local area knowledge.
- Access to reliable transaction or rental intelligence.
- Strong agent or auction sourcing network.
- Ability to judge management and resident profile.
- Renovation execution skill.
- Financing discipline and cash reserve.
- Patience to wait for mispricing.
- Ability to compare substitutes objectively.
- Ability to walk away after spending time on a deal.

A claimed edge is valid only when it helps the user buy better, operate better, finance safer, or exit stronger than ordinary buyers.

## Refusal And Cooling-Off Rules

EstateLab should refuse to encourage a user when:

- They have no emergency fund.
- They cannot survive vacancy or repair costs.
- They rely on optimistic rent to pay debt.
- They do not understand the property or transaction.
- They want to ignore caveat, title, bankability, management, or hard-stop issues.
- They are buying from social pressure or group excitement.
- Their strategy depends on a misleading financing structure.
- They cannot explain why a future tenant or buyer would choose the property.

When emotional pressure is high, EstateLab should recommend a cooling-off period and require comparison against substitutes before proceeding.

## User Onboarding Outputs

After profiling, EstateLab should output:

- Investor profile label.
- Allowed strategies.
- Restricted strategies.
- Blocked strategies.
- Required cash buffer.
- Maximum comfortable purchase range.
- Required rental coverage.
- Maximum tolerable negative cash flow.
- Minimum evidence standard.
- Main likely mistake.
- One rule EstateLab will enforce most strictly for this user.

The profile should be updated when the user's income, cash reserve, debt, knowledge, or experience changes.
