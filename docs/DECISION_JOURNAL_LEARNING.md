# Decision Journal And Second-Brain Learning

## Scope Of This File

This file defines how EstateLab records a decision before the outcome, reviews what happened, distinguishes skill from luck, and updates beliefs.

The purpose is not to prove the founder was right. The purpose is to make future judgment better.

The central question is:

> What did we believe before the outcome, why did we believe it, what actually happened, and what should change?

## Founder Pre-Purchase Targets

Current founder assumptions and targets include:

- Net ROI of at least 6%.
- Capital appreciation target of 10% within one year.
- Entry preference of at least 20% below the relevant median or defensible market value.
- Holding period of about seven to eight years.
- Future exit to both owner-occupiers and investors.

EstateLab should preserve these as founder targets, not universal market truths.

## Return Definition Discipline

The phrase **net ROI of 6%** is not precise enough for a decision record. Before using it, EstateLab must identify the formula:

- Net rental yield on purchase price.
- Net rental yield on all-in cost.
- Cash-on-cash return.
- Return on current equity.
- Total return including appreciation.

This matters because the founder previously used a 6% gross rental-yield threshold. Six percent gross yield and six percent net ROI are materially different standards and must never be treated as interchangeable.

The decision journal should record the numerator, denominator, included costs, and measurement period.

## One-Year Appreciation Target

The founder considers 10% capital appreciation within one year a successful investment outcome.

EstateLab should treat this as an upside target or early market-validation signal, not the sole definition of success.

Reasons:

- Malaysian sales conditions may be stagnant.
- Property transactions are slow and costly.
- A one-year value estimate may rely on asking prices or bank valuation rather than completed resale evidence.
- A seven-to-eight-year holding thesis should not be judged entirely by first-year price movement.
- Buying below market may create immediate equity without proving that the broader market appreciated.

EstateLab should separate:

- **Entry discount**: value captured at purchase.
- **Market appreciation**: comparable market value increasing after purchase.
- **Value added**: improvement caused by renovation, management, or execution.
- **Realized gain**: net proceeds after an actual sale and all costs.

A property can be successful without 10% first-year appreciation when rental demand, holding power, buyer depth, and long-term thesis remain strong. Conversely, a paper gain based only on optimistic valuation is not yet a successful outcome.

## Saleability As The Main Blind Spot

The founder identifies saleability as:

- The most difficult assumption to verify before purchase.
- A risk naturally underestimated when the property is attractive.
- The criticism most likely to change the decision.

EstateLab should therefore make saleability a mandatory pre-purchase pre-mortem.

Before buying, record:

- Natural future owner-occupier.
- Natural future investor.
- Expected selling price and financeable buyer income.
- Closest substitute properties.
- Most likely buyer objection.
- Expected time to sell in normal and weak markets.
- Price reduction needed to create liquidity.
- Unit-level objections that cannot be removed.

Saleability is not binary. Almost any legal property may sell at some price. The useful question is whether it can sell within the intended time, discount, and buyer pool.

## Unit Placement Lesson

The founder's first-purchase lesson was accepting an inferior unit position because the entry price was cheaper. A unit near a refuse room or passenger lift can be harder to sell.

EstateLab should permanently check:

- Refuse room or refuse route.
- Passenger and service lifts.
- Lift lobby traffic and noise.
- Mechanical or electrical rooms.
- Pump rooms and other service areas.
- Car-park access and headlight exposure.
- Facility, road, commercial, or loading-bay noise.
- Privacy and corridor traffic.

A discount is acceptable only when it compensates for the likely rental and resale penalty. An unchangeable unit-location weakness should be compared against better-positioned units in the same project before purchase.

## Build Quality And Management Warning

The founder observes that excited buyers commonly dismiss build quality and management quality.

EstateLab should treat these as structural rather than cosmetic concerns because they affect:

- Resident experience.
- Repair cost.
- Project image.
- Tenant retention.
- Financing and buyer confidence.
- Long-term saleability.

Cheap entry does not repair a deteriorating building or ineffective management.

## Evidence, Data, And Site Visit

The founder prefers accurate data from reliable sources and applies a data-first approach before personal perception. The founder also considers site-visit vibe more important than cold numbers in every case.

These statements create a useful tension, but both are too absolute.

Data can be:

- Accurate but lagging.
- Correct for the wrong comparable.
- Incomplete or selectively reported.
- Based on asking rather than achieved outcomes.
- Distorted by incentives, bulk sales, or unusual transactions.

Site-visit intuition can detect noise, smell, privacy, resident behaviour, maintenance, security, layout friction, and neighbourhood feel that structured data misses. It can also be influenced by mood, staging, weather, confirmation bias, and one unusually good or bad visit.

EstateLab's rule is:

> Use data to establish the market case, then use site visits to test lived quality. When they disagree, investigate the disagreement rather than automatically choosing either one.

The statement that intuition has never been wrong cannot be verified without predictions recorded before outcomes. EstateLab should treat it as an untested confidence claim, not established evidence.

## Evidence Packet Before Purchase

The minimum evidence packet should include:

- Completed subsale transactions.
- Successful auction bids where relevant.
- Verified achieved or strongly triangulated rent.
- Current competing supply and occupancy evidence.
- Project management and maintenance evidence.
- Title, restriction, caveat, seller, and transaction checks.
- Unit floor-plan position and physical inspection.
- Financing and valuation terms.
- Closest alternative properties.
- Clear data dates and sources.

No dataset is accepted merely because it contains numbers. EstateLab should record source reliability, recency, scope, and known gaps.

## Kill Criteria

Founder hard stops include:

- A transaction that appears suspicious or inconsistent.
- A material change in title or legal status.
- Fundamental property weakness that price alone cannot repair.

EstateLab should classify a problem as **solvable** only when it is:

- Within the buyer's control.
- Legally and technically clear.
- Reliably costed.
- Fixable within an acceptable time.
- Unlikely to return.
- Compensated by the price.
- Unlikely to damage future bankability or saleability.

Examples may include ordinary cosmetic renovation, missing furniture, minor repair, or correctable presentation.

Walk away when the problem is outside the buyer's control or permanently attached to the asset, including unresolved title risk, suspicious transaction structure, structural defect, poor unit placement, weak management, hostile restrictions, poor access, narrow buyer pool, or broken demand thesis.

The founder's principle is sound:

> The property is the main subject. Price is only the transaction medium.

A lower price can compensate for measurable inconvenience or cost. It cannot transform a structurally weak asset into a high-quality one.

## Booking Fee And Better Alternatives

The founder may withdraw after paying a booking fee if a better alternative appears.

EstateLab should compare:

- Recoverability or forfeiture of the booking fee.
- Legal terms and deadlines.
- The expected long-term difference between the properties.
- Switching transaction cost.
- Whether the new alternative is genuinely better or simply newer and more exciting.

A booking fee is a sunk cost once unrecoverable, but that does not make switching automatically correct. The expected benefit of the alternative should materially exceed the switching loss and uncertainty.

## Review Timing

The founder wants the first outcome review immediately after the unit is rented.

EstateLab should begin observation earlier and perform formal reviews at:

1. Completion or acquisition.
2. Start of rental marketing.
3. First signed tenancy.
4. Three months if the unit remains vacant or underperforms.
5. Every tenancy renewal.
6. Annually for value, competition, cost, and thesis.
7. Before refinancing, further acquisition, or sale.

The first tenancy review compares expected rent, achieved rent, furnishing cost, tenant profile, vacancy duration, agent feedback, and true monthly cash flow.

## Success Without Appreciation

Consistent rental demand can validate a good decision even when the property has not appreciated.

EstateLab should check whether:

- Achieved net return matches the recorded assumption.
- Vacancy remains within tolerance.
- Tenant quality and renewal are acceptable.
- Maintenance and repairs remain controlled.
- The property retains saleability and competitive position.

Rental success does not automatically validate the appreciation thesis, but it can validate the holding-power and product-demand thesis.

## Decision Quality Versus Financial Outcome

The founder asks how a decision can be bad if the property made money.

A profitable outcome can follow a poor decision when:

- The investor ignored a serious known risk.
- The result depended on an unexpected market boom.
- An unsupported valuation happened to rise.
- Excessive leverage survived only because no stress event occurred.
- A weak property was rescued by luck, policy, or a buyer paying irrationally.

A good decision can also experience a poor outcome because of a genuinely unforeseeable event.

EstateLab should score separately:

- **Process quality**: evidence, comparison, reasoning, stress testing, and rule compliance.
- **Outcome quality**: rent, appreciation, costs, liquidity, and total return.
- **Luck contribution**: external events not reasonably predicted or controlled.
- **Execution quality**: renovation, tenant selection, management, refinancing, and exit actions.

Profit matters, but it does not prove that the original reasoning was repeatable.

## Skill Versus Luck

The founder defines skill as being able to justify the action and not relying solely on the market to move the price.

EstateLab accepts this and should also test:

- Whether the thesis was recorded before the result.
- Whether the outcome followed the predicted causal chain.
- Whether controllable execution added value.
- Whether similar decisions perform consistently across several cases.
- Whether the result beat realistic alternatives after risk and cost.

An explanation written after the outcome is not proof of skill unless it matches the pre-purchase record.

## Framework Fallibility

The founder suggests that following the entire framework should eliminate post-purchase mistakes.

EstateLab rejects this claim.

The framework can reduce avoidable errors, but it cannot eliminate:

- Incomplete information.
- Fraud or concealed defects.
- Policy and credit changes.
- Economic shocks.
- Tenant behaviour.
- Management deterioration.
- Incorrect assumptions.
- Poor execution after purchase.
- Rare events.

Following the framework is evidence of process discipline, not immunity from mistakes. Every meaningful outcome still requires review, including profitable ones.

## Testable Rental-Quality Belief

The founder proposes that a bad property in a good location can still generate healthy rental return because tenants may accept lower quality when the price is right.

EstateLab should treat this as an open, segment-specific hypothesis.

Test it through:

- Achieved rent rather than asking rent.
- Net return after the price concession and higher repair cost.
- Vacancy and tenant turnover.
- Tenant quality and unit condition.
- Comparison with better-managed substitutes.
- Renewal rate.
- Resale liquidity.

If lower rent is required to compensate for poor quality, the property may remain rentable while still underperforming on net return, tenant quality, and saleability.

## Testable Saleability Belief

The founder's uncertain belief is that only good properties are readily disposable, while poorly managed properties struggle to sell even when rental demand is strong.

EstateLab should refine this:

> Better-quality properties should retain a broader buyer pool and require a smaller liquidity discount, while poorly managed properties may still sell if price falls enough or yield attracts investors.

Test saleability through completed transactions, time on market, financing success, price reduction, bidder depth, owner-occupier participation, and performance against substitutes.

## Belief Update Standard

EstateLab should weight contrary evidence by quality, relevance, and independence rather than count alone.

Default review protocol:

- One severe, credible counterexample reopens the belief immediately.
- Three independent in-scope counterexamples trigger a formal confidence review.
- Five consistent in-scope counterexamples normally require revising, narrowing, or retiring the belief unless stronger evidence explains the difference.

A large dataset with poor comparability should not outweigh one highly relevant completed case. The review should identify whether the belief was wrong, too broad, applied to the wrong segment, or used under different market conditions.

## Conflict Between Experience And External Evidence

The founder prefers to skip a purchase when uncertainty remains unresolved.

EstateLab accepts this as a strong default.

When experience conflicts with external evidence:

1. Check whether definitions, dates, geography, property type, and buyer segment match.
2. Check source reliability and incentives.
3. Search for a third independent source or direct observation.
4. Lower confidence rather than force agreement.
5. Skip the purchase when the conflict could materially damage the outcome and cannot be resolved.

## Independent Judgment

The founder's final principle is to trust personal judgment over external opinion, own mistakes, learn, and continue.

EstateLab should refine it to:

> Make the final decision independently because only the investor fully knows their circumstances, but require personal judgment and external advice to face the same evidence tests.

Blindly following others is dangerous. Automatically trusting oneself is also dangerous. Independence means taking responsibility for a decision that has survived challenge.

The permanent warning is not merely to “refer to the framework.” It is:

> Do not bypass the framework because confidence, FOMO, cheap price, authority, or a previous success makes the answer feel obvious.

## Mandatory Pre-Purchase Decision Record

Before EstateLab supports a purchase, record:

1. Property, geography, price, and all-in cost.
2. Investor mandate and intended portfolio role.
3. Expected rent, vacancy, net-return formula, and holding cost.
4. Entry discount and evidence-supported fair value.
5. Appreciation target, timeline, and causal driver.
6. Planned holding period and exit year.
7. Future owner-occupier and investor buyer pools.
8. Supporting evidence with source and date.
9. Assumptions with confidence levels.
10. Strongest counter-thesis.
11. Closest alternatives.
12. Saleability pre-mortem.
13. Kill criteria.
14. Decision and review date.

## Mandatory Outcome Record

At each review, record:

1. What was expected by this date.
2. What actually happened.
3. Which assumptions were correct or wrong.
4. Whether the causal thesis worked.
5. Process, execution, outcome, and luck scores.
6. New contrary evidence.
7. Beliefs to retain, lower, narrow, revise, or retire.
8. The next decision or review trigger.

## Stage 7 Protection Rule

> Record the prediction before the outcome, judge the process separately from the profit, and update confidence when evidence disagrees. A framework is valuable only when it can reveal that its owner was wrong.
